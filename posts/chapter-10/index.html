Chapter 10

November 7, 2024 · 6 min  | 

Brynn's knees buckled to the ground, forcing him to awkwardly drop Seleeku down on the cold stone floor of Grimcairn. He was dimly aware of his surroundings. A large round room, barren except for a lidless empty stone coffin in the middle.

There was blood. Too much blood.

Seleeku’s eyes opened ever so slightly, thin slits that were just enough to expose clouded pupils. She spoke a word so faintly that Brynn wasn’t sure he heard it or imagined it.

“… don’t …”

Her eyes closed. Was she still breathing?

“No-no-no-no …” Brynn kept repeating the word as if it might stop what was happening.

“We can save her, you know,” said a familiar voice from behind him.

“Kodroth!” Brynn exclaimed, turning to face him. “Am I in a dream?” His voice cracked with hope.

“No, Backstabber, you’re not. You’re, um, having a moment.” Kodroth almost sounded embarrassed.

“We can save her? How?”

“Well, here’s the thing, Backstabber. If we’re to do this, I need you to promise me something. When the time comes, I need you to hear me out.”

“Hear you out?” Brynn asked, confused.

“Hear everything I have to say. That’s all I ask.”

Brynn looked at Seleeku and then back at Kodroth, indecision gnawing at his insides. Something was wrong. He knew it.

“She’s fading, Backstabber, quickly.”

“Alright! Yes! Yes! I’ll do what you ask! I promise!” Brynn shouted.

“I knew you’d make the right choice,” Kodroth said with barely contained glee. “All I need you to do is place her in there.” He nodded at the coffin. “I’ll take care of the rest.”

The coffin. It was made of black polished granite and gave him an ill feeling. Brynn picked up Seleeku as gingerly as he could and reluctantly carried her over to it. He looked down at her face, pale but somehow peaceful. He could still change his mind.

A thin rivelet of blood trickled past her lips. He put those thoughts aside. It was time. He gently set her down inside. Her last breath escaped with a wheeze and her body went still …

… She gasped, her lungs filling with air, with life, and let out a painful wail. A dark keen full of sadness and despair and aching. Brynn dropped to his knees and put his hands over his ears, but the sound pierced through them and into his mind, drowning out all else. He was in a void, falling, falling, falling. A numbness took hold. A finality. He had no more thoughts.

He opened his eyes.

He was laying on the ground. Kodroth was gone. Seleeku was sitting up in the coffin, looking up at nothing, tears streaming down her cheeks.

“What have I done?!” she cried.

“You saved my life!, I-I—” Brynn stammered as he pushed himself to a standing position. “I … What did you do?”

“The prophecies …” she sobbed.

“The Old Ones?” he asked, involuntarily taking a step back. The Old Ones had written down their prophecies, but the Druids of Galdir would never read them. They were cursed.

She didn’t answer. Instead, she asked, “Nessana?” Her voice quavered.

“It all happened so fast. I’m sure they made it to the forest, though. Are you alright?”

Seleeku pulled herself out of the coffin and stood on Grimcairn’s stone floor. Her legs trembled and she swayed back and forth unsteadily. Her clothes were still crimson where the wyvern had wounded her, but she was now unharmed. It was a miracle. A dark and terrible miracle.

“We have to find them!”

Brynn placed his hands on her shoulders to steady her. Maybe himself too. “Seleeku, you’re in no condition to do anything just yet. And the wyverns could still be out there. Let’s rest for a moment.”

Seleeku nodded vaguely and slumped down on to the ground. Her carefree grace was gone. “You’ve made a corrupt bargain,” she stated, hugging her knees and staring downward.

“W-What makes you say that?” Brynn asked, surprised. “Do the elves have their own prophecies?”

She let out a bitter laugh. “Yes, you could say that.”

So he studied the room instead. It was round with the walls made of the same kind of rocks that were on the outer side of Grimcairn. They curved upwards, forming a dome above his head. If the magic that held all the stones together were to disappear, Brynn and Seleeku would be crushed immediately by the full weight of the barrow collapsing down upon them. It was not a pleasant thought.

A dim glow suffused the room, as if by a dull moon, but Brynn couldn’t discern where it came from. Everything felt like it was covered in one big shadow. The effect was disquieting, making the interior space seem like the scene of a bad dream. But it wasn’t a dream—according to Kodroth, at least.

Kodroth had told him that the answers to some of his question lay here, but where? And which questions? The room was empty except for the stone coffin, which was absent of any markings. And the Supreme Warlock was nowhere to be found. As far as Brynn could tell, he might never have visited this place.

“You won’t find any answers here,” Seleeku spoke softly.

“But that’s not what …” Brynn trailed off. “Tell me about the prophecies,” he asked instead.

Seleeku gave Brynn a long, hard stare. Finally she replied, “When I was seven, I remember crying to my mother that I had dreams of her leaving me alone in a forest. She told me they were just nightmares. But they wouldn’t go away. I couldn’t make them go away. Three months later and—” she paused, not wanting to remember, “—and she was gone. She went on an expedition in the Far Forest and disappeared. We never found out what happened to her. But I know, I know she’s gone forever.”

“Sometimes I have visions,” she continued. “Or a voice speaks in my head. Other times it’s just a feeling.”

“The prophecies, they’re yours?” Brynn asked. He felt himself shiver.

She spoke in a mournful voice:

The one who sleeps beneath the Hollow’s Tree,
 the one who crosses the River for thee,
 the one who plants the acorns three,
 will save your life and set you free.

“That’s—”

“—a terrible rhyme, I know. They usually are,” Seleeku said.

“—about me?!” Brynn’s voice was shrill.
